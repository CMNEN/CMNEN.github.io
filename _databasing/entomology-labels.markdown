The labels on our beetle specimens are almost important as the beetle itself. Without any labels, much of the scientific value of a specimen is lost. At the CMNEN, you will often see beetle specimens with 1) a specimen/"data" label and 2) a determination label. These labels are typically made out of heavy stock, acid-free paper that will not deteriorate over time. Sometimes, you will find cyrobank voucher labels or labels indicating that a particular specimen was part of a major donation from a private collection.

## Specimen/"data" labels

![Image of specimen label](/assets/spec-label.png)
![Second image of specimen label from a specimen collected for the EMP project](/assets/emp-label.png)

These labels are typical examples of specimen/"data" labels. Most of such labels will include information relating to the site that the specimen was collected from, the date the specimen was collected, and the name of the collectors. These examples pictured above also provide several other important pieces of information that should be captured during the process of databasing such as the habitat where the specimen was colelcted in (Second label: "deciduous forest"), elevation (First label: "700m"), and collection method (First label: "Malaise trap w/pans").


## Determination labels

![Image of determination label](/assets/det-label.png)

This determination label shows that the specimen associated with it was identified ("determined") as _Dorcus_ (_Metallactulus_) _sakanashii_ by M. J. Paulsen in 2023. Mizunuma is stated as the author for the species, and the authorship year is 1994. The parentheses around the author and the authorship year indicate that this is not the orignal taxonomic placement or combination. If you are entering data in Excel, this bit of information would go under the columns "Scientific_name", "Determiner", and "Det_date". refer to the page "Column Key" for more information.

Ideally, every specimen would have its own determination label, but this is not always the case. In many instances, only the first specimen in a series bears a determination label that is understood to apply to the remaining specimens in the series (see image above). If you see this in the collection, make sure the series stays together and that the original order of specimens in the unit tray is maintained.

## Catalogue labels

![Image of catalogue label](/assets/cat-label.png)

This is an example of a catalogue label. Specimens that possess a catalogue label are recorded in our database, as catalogue labels are only applied once a specimen has been databased and all associated information has been entered and linked to a unique catalogue number. Each catalogue label includes a catalogue number (in this case "CMNEN 00085571"), which consists of our collection code (“CMNEN”) followed by a unique catalogue number (“00085571”). The QR code on the label corresponds to a Data Matrix that was historically linked to records in MANTIS. This functionality is not currently in use.
